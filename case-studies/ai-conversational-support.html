Work / Conversational support

LLM-powered conversational support platform

Internal chat experience to deflect repetitive platform questions—natural language understanding, guided (not fully open-ended) flows, and clean escalation to humans. Built 0→1 for 10,000+ employees with web and Slack entry points.

Role Product manager · Lead UI/UX
Scope 0→1 product
Users 10,000+ employees
My role

I worked as product manager and lead UI/UX designer on this initiative from discovery through delivery and design QA, alongside engineering, data, and stakeholders as described later in this case study.

TL;DR

As product manager and lead UI/UX designer on a 0→1 LLM-powered internal support experience for 10,000+ Nike platform users. Chat-first flows, guided responses, and human escalation replaced the slow ticket treadmill—cutting repetitive queries ~65%, improving response time ~75% (roughly 4–6 hours → under an hour), reducing support cost ~45%, and handling 15,000+ conversations in the first three months while keeping trust explicit.

Problem & context

Employees leaned on ticket-based support for everyday platform questions—workflows, policies, troubleshooting. Median wait was 4–6 hours; thorny issues stretched to days. Support was drowning in 500+ tickets per day, roughly 70% of which were repetitive “how do I…” work that documentation failed to make self-serve. There was no unified conversational path that balanced speed, accuracy, and privacy for internal users.

Why this mattered: automate the routine without trapping employees in open-ended AI—so support could focus on complex cases while everyone else got faster, clearer answers.

Role & responsibilities

  • I owned end-to-end UX strategy for the conversational platform—linking product direction, trust model, and measurable business goals.
  • I defined the AI interaction model: guided vs. open-ended surfaces, escalation triggers, accuracy thresholds, and what “done” meant for each intent.
  • I partnered with ML engineers to position the LLM as an intent parser and composer—not an autonomous decision-maker for policy or access.
  • I aligned Product, Engineering, and Support leadership on trade-offs between automation, risk, and human oversight.
  • I designed conversational flows for mixed technical literacy—plain language, predictable scaffolding, and recovery when the model was unsure.
  • I shipped admin dashboards for monitoring, tuning, and ownership so Support could iterate without filing tickets against themselves.
  • I facilitated cross-functional reviews and workshops to keep roadmap bets grounded in real ticket taxonomy—not demo lore.
  • I championed transparency patterns—disclosure of limits, provenance where available, and respectful handoff copy—to keep employee confidence high.

Constraints

  • LLM accuracy limits and messy edge cases for internal policies.
  • Consistent, helpful tone across intents and languages of support.
  • Strict privacy expectations for employee questions and metadata.
  • Integration with existing ticketing, knowledge bases, and Slack workflows.
  • Wide variance in technical literacy across the employee base.
  • Limited high-quality training signal for niche platform questions.

Research & insights

I analyzed 10,000+ historical tickets to cluster recurring questions, interviewed 50+ employees on expectations and fear patterns, and shadowed support to watch how edge cases were actually resolved.

Ticket reality

Most volume was navigational and procedural—perfect for guided automation if we surfaced confidence and next steps honestly.

Trust reality

Employees tolerated slower answers more than wrong answers; predictable escalation rebuilt credibility faster than “magical” completions.

  • Benchmarked conversational patterns (ChatGPT, Intercom) for tone, pacing, and disclosure—not to copy consumer guardrails wholesale.
  • Prototype-tested with employees across rounds, iterating on clarity, fallback copy, and admin visibility into failure modes.
  • Created lightweight personas for distinct support needs (new hire, power user, field vs. HQ) to stress-test flows.

Design process

  1. Mapped and categorized the highest-volume employee questions from ticket data.
  2. Designed a conversational shell with task-based structure so users always knew what the assistant could do in-channel.
  3. Authored guided response patterns instead of unconstrained freeform chat for the highest-risk intents.
  4. Prototyped LLM-assisted understanding flows with engineering—streaming states, latency, and retrieval failure UX included.
  5. Built escalation paths to human agents with context handoff so support did not restart from zero.
  6. Validated usefulness and perceived accuracy with structured employee tests.
  7. Added feedback loops (thumbs, reason codes) feeding review queues for continuous improvement.
  8. Delivered an admin dashboard for monitoring volume, accuracy, and escalation quality.
  9. Shipped onboarding that introduced chat support where employees already worked— including Slack alongside web—to reduce activation friction.

Key decisions

  • Chat-first, not ticket-first — Met employees in a conversational surface for routine answers instead of forcing another queue ID before help started.
  • LLM for NLU, humans for judgment — Used the model to parse intent and assemble grounded responses while reserving policy and edge cases for people.
  • Guided, not fully open-ended — Constrained high-risk intents to scripted guardrails with optional LLM phrasing—reducing hallucination risk while still feeling conversational.
  • Escalation as a product feature — Designed human handoff as a first-class success path with transcripts, metadata, and SLA-aware routing.

The goal was trustworthy acceleration: speed where automation is safe, humans where nuance or liability dominates—without hiding the seams.

How I worked with engineering, ML, support & legal

  • I co-defined safe intents, retrieval contracts, and escalation triggers with Support—mapping flows to operational SLAs, not slide decks.
  • I paired with ML and platform engineers on streaming UI, timeouts, logging, and evaluation hooks so UX reflected real model behavior.
  • I ran working sessions with legal/comms on disclosures, retention, and what the assistant could claim vs. defer.

What we cut (on purpose)

A launch-week “ask anything” free chat. It would have exploded failure modes and poisoned trust. We shipped bounded intents first, expanded only where monitoring showed stable accuracy, and kept escalation obvious.

What shipped

A conversational support platform on web and Slack following User query → LLM understanding → guided response → optional human escalation. Employees ask in natural language; the system returns task-shaped answers with transparent limits, feedback capture, and admin tooling for monitoring quality over time.

Outcomes

Metrics combine support operations reporting, product analytics on conversations, finance rollups on cost to serve, and post-launch surveys. They describe program-level impact across the internal footprint, not a single pilot cohort.

65% reduction in support queries handled manually (program tracking)
75% faster responses (roughly 4–6 hours → under one hour median)
45% support cost reduction from automating routine inquiries
15,000+ conversations in the first three months after launch
82% measured accuracy on LLM-handled responses (evaluation set)
62% → 88% employee satisfaction with support (post-launch survey)
Capacity freed specialists to focus on complex, high-touch incidents
24/7 availability for routine questions across global time zones

Learnings

  • Guided responses outperform unconstrained chat for regulated internal scenarios—users want clarity on what the assistant can safely do.
  • Treat the LLM as an interpreter and composer, not the system of record—pair it with vetted content and human pathways.
  • Escalation is a trust feature; design the handoff with as much care as the happy path.
  • Conversational UI still needs familiar affordances—typing alone is not enough when people are stressed.
  • Meeting employees in Slack (and similar surfaces) accelerates adoption more than a net-new destination alone.
