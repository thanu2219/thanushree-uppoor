AI image & video generation platform

AI-powered creative production for Nike marketing and creative teams— 10,000+ employees in scope—replacing slow vendor cycles with guided generation, templates, brand-aligned presets, and export-ready packaging for imagery and video at scale.

Role Lead UX designer
Reach 10,000+ employees
Partners Brand, legal, MLOps
My role

I led UX and UI on this work from discovery through delivery and design QA, alongside product, engineering, and stakeholders as described later in this case study.

TL;DR

Lead UX/UI designer on Nike’s AI image and video generation platform for marketing and creative ops. I shipped guided prompts, templates, brand-aligned presets, and export-ready workflows so teams could move faster without losing control—roughly 60% faster end-to-end production (weeks → days), 10× iteration on variations, ~70% lower production cost for targeted asset types, 15,000+ assets in the first six months, and adoption climbing from 12% → 68%.

Problem & context

Campaign work depended on thousands of product images and promos, but the default path was slow vendor loops: 2–3 week turnarounds, high per-asset cost (often $500–$2,000 depending on complexity), and little room to A/B test creative or react to market shifts. Internal teams needed an AI-assisted path that still respected Nike brand, compliance, and downstream production realities—not a playground that created unowned files.

Why this mattered: accelerate creative production and reduce cost while keeping brand, rights, and reproducibility non-negotiable.

Role & responsibilities

  • I led UX design for the AI-powered creative generation platform—from first workflows through shipped interaction patterns.
  • I collaborated with ML engineers and creative stakeholders to align model capabilities with what the UI honestly promised.
  • I designed interaction patterns for AI-assisted workflows: generate, compare, refine, and package with traceable intent.
  • I built interfaces for non-technical creatives—progressive disclosure so power users could move fast without overwhelming occasional users.
  • I established trust and transparency patterns: model limits, why a generation failed, what changed between versions, and what is safe to ship.

Constraints

  • Model variability and unpredictable outputs for certain prompts.
  • Strict Nike brand guidelines and review expectations.
  • Compliance requirements for generated content and usage rights.
  • Wide range of technical literacy across marketing and creative roles.

Research & insights

I interviewed 30+ creative team members, mapped production workflows end to end, and ran co-design sessions with creatives to learn where speed helped vs. where it created rework.

Ops reality

Adoption hinged on exports matching real toolchain needs—naming, color, format, and rights metadata were part of the creative product, not “admin.”

Brand reality

Guardrails had to be explainable; opaque refusals pushed teams back to vendors and side channels.

  • Benchmarked consumer AI tools (Midjourney, DALL-E patterns) for prompting and review behaviors—then adapted for enterprise accountability.
  • Identified where templates beat freeform prompts: repeatable campaign archetypes, seasonal bundles, and product-family conventions.

Design process

  1. Mapped end-to-end creative production from brief to export handoff.
  2. Designed simplified prompt surfaces with guardrails, examples, and safe defaults—not a blank chat box as the only entry point.
  3. Created template systems for common scenarios so teams could start from “known good” configurations.
  4. Prototyped brand guideline enforcement in-flow: checks, warnings, and human-readable reasons when outputs were out of bounds.
  5. Built asset management capabilities: versions, comparisons, packaging, and export presets aligned to downstream tools.

Key decisions

  • Ship a production platform, not a demo — Prioritized flows that could survive campaign deadlines: versioning, compare, and explicit packaging beats “wow first frame.”
  • AI-assisted, human-accountable — Designed interactions where the model accelerates iteration but humans retain approval, rights, and release decisions.
  • Templates before raw prompting — Made the fastest path the safest path for non-technical users; advanced controls unlock only when needed.

Creative velocity only compounds if outputs are reviewable, reproducible, and defensible—otherwise AI becomes another source of throwaway files.

How I worked with brand, legal & MLOps

  • I co-defined allowed / restricted patterns with legal and mapped them to UI states creatives could interpret without reading policy PDFs.
  • I partnered with MLOps on model transparency: what changed between runs, how to retry safely, and how to roll back a batch when quality regressed.
  • I ran critiques with brand designers on gallery, diff, and approval patterns so review rituals matched how teams already shipped creative.

What we cut (on purpose)

An infinite canvas “explore anything” mode for v1. It demoed well but produced untracked asset sprawl. Exploration stayed inside versioned sessions with explicit compare and packaging steps so outputs could be audited and handed off.

What shipped

An intuitive creative surface for generating product imagery and video through guided prompts, templates, and brand-aligned presets. Users upload product inputs, generate multiple variations, refine with compare/diff workflows, and export production-ready files with brand safety checks and rights-aware packaging.

Outcomes

Metrics combine production cycle tracking (brief to export), finance rollups on vendor vs. internal cost, asset telemetry, and adoption analytics—reported at the program level for participating marketing and creative teams.

60% faster end-to-end production (roughly 2–3 weeks → 2–3 days for supported flows)
10× faster iteration across creative variations (pilot cohort measurement)
70% lower production cost for eligible asset types (finance estimate)
15,000+ assets generated in the first six months
12% → 68% active adoption of the AI tooling (program analytics)
Vendor load reduced for routine creative production; vendors focused on highest-complexity work

Learnings

  • AI UX should hide complexity for first-time success while preserving advanced controls for experts—progressive disclosure beats a single “expert mode.”
  • Templates beat empty prompts for repeatable enterprise creative; they encode brand and reduce error rates.
  • Trust requires transparency about model limits, failure reasons, and what changed between versions.
  • Brand safety has to live inside the workflow—not as a post-export policing step.
