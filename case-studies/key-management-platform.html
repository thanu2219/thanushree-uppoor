Key management platform

Simplified permissions and secure access workflows for Nike’s enterprise key management—10,000+ employees and hundreds of internal systems—with role-based templates, guided requests, and approver experiences that made least-privilege the path of least resistance.

Role Lead UX designer
Reach 10,000+ employees
Focus Access · approvals · audit
My role

I was the sole designer for UX and UI on this work—discovery through delivery and design QA—alongside product, engineering, and stakeholders as described later in this case study.

TL;DR

Sole UX/UI design lead on the redesign of Nike’s enterprise key management experience for 10,000+ employees and hundreds of internal systems. I shipped role-based templates, guided request flows, approver surfaces with real context, and audit-ready reporting—cutting access-related errors ~55%, improving approval cycle time ~50% (roughly 5–7 days → 2–3 days), and lifting onboarding clarity ~40% while reducing noisy back-and-forth on tickets.

Problem & context

Nike’s access platform governed permissions across a sprawling internal estate, but the experience pushed complexity onto employees and managers. People had to parse opaque permission hierarchies just to ask for the right thing— 45% of requests bounced for clarification or resubmission, and approvals often stretched 5–7 days. That friction created real security risk (over-broad asks, rubber-stamp patterns) and real productivity drag.

Why this mattered: tighten the loop between request, evidence, and decision so security improves because the UX is clearer—not in spite of it.

Role & responsibilities

  • I led the end-to-end UX redesign of the enterprise access management platform—from IA through shipped UI patterns.
  • I partnered with Security, IT, and HR so policy, identity plumbing, and people data stayed aligned to what we could responsibly show in product.
  • I designed simplified request and approval flows that reduced guesswork while preserving least-privilege intent.
  • I ran research with employees, managers, and administrators to map failure modes, workarounds, and “shadow permission” habits.
  • I introduced role-based templates and intelligent recommendations so common bundles were one honest tap—not a paragraph of security jargon.
  • I delivered audit and reporting views that helped compliance teams trust the system—not fight it—during reviews.

Constraints

  • Strict security, compliance, and separation-of-duty expectations.
  • Integration with legacy identity and workforce systems.
  • Complex, nested organizational roles and exception paths.
  • Immutable-feeling audit trails and exportable evidence for audits.

Research & insights

I interviewed 50+ employees, managers, and IT administrators, analyzed request and error patterns, and mapped journeys for common access scenarios—from new hire provisioning to elevated break-glass needs.

Approver reality

Approvers optimized for throughput when context was missing—creating recurring “fix-forward” incidents that were expensive to unwind.

Requester reality

People copied old tickets or asked for “max access” because precise scopes felt risky; templates had to make narrow access feel safer than broad access.

  • Benchmarked enterprise access patterns (Okta, SailPoint) for disclosure, escalation, and admin ergonomics—not to clone, but to pressure-test our IA.
  • Synthesized error taxonomies into design requirements: which failures should be prevented vs. explained vs. escalated.

Design process

  1. Mapped information architecture for role-based access templates and how they mapped to live org data.
  2. Designed simplified request forms with contextual help, progressive disclosure, and explicit scope previews.
  3. Built intelligent recommendations from role, team, and peer patterns—always with an escape hatch to custom scopes when justified.
  4. Prototyped approval workflows that surfaced blast radius, peers-with-access, and dependency cues for approvers.
  5. Designed audit and reporting views for compliance consumers—status, history, and export paths that matched review cadences.

Key decisions

  • Redesign the workflow, not just the chrome — Rebuilt request → evidence → decision → audit as a coherent story instead of bolting widgets onto the old mental model.
  • Templates + recommendations as the default path — Made correct-by-construction bundles easier than free-text guesses that triggered resubmissions.
  • Contextual approval, not queue-only approval — Mandated the minimum viable context panel for decisions that previously relied on tribal knowledge and side channels.

Security improved when we reduced honest mistakes and made the “safe path” the fast path—without hiding the underlying policy rules from people who need them.

How I worked with security, IT & engineering

  • I co-mapped break-glass, rotation, and revocation journeys with security architects so rare paths shipped with the same rigor as happy paths.
  • I defined UI states for partial provisioning, async approvals, and failure recovery with engineering—no faux-instant states that violated backend truth.
  • I ran usability tests with approvers on anonymized scenarios and fed themes back into policy discussions—not only into component spacing.

What we cut (on purpose)

A consumer-style “approve all unrelated requests” shortcut. It tested fast in labs, but it recreated the exact batch-risk pattern we were trying to eliminate—we limited batching to explicitly grouped, risk-aligned bundles only.

What shipped

A redesigned key management experience with role-based templates that pre-populate common permission sets, guided request forms with intelligent suggestions, and approver views that carry the context required to say yes or no with confidence. Employees get clear status, reminders where appropriate, and audit-friendly histories so compliance teams can trace decisions without opening tickets.

Outcomes

Metrics combine ticket analytics (resubmissions, IT support volume), cycle-time measurements on approvals, error classification from security operations, and survey signals—reported at the program level for the access domain.

55% reduction in access-related errors (post-redesign tracking)
50% faster approvals (roughly 5–7 days → 2–3 days median cycle)
+40% onboarding clarity lift from structured templates and guidance
45% → 12% resubmission / clarification rate on access requests
86% user satisfaction in post-launch survey sample
38% fewer IT support tickets for access issues (ticket trend)

Learnings

  • You can simplify without weakening security—translate policy into defaults, templates, and recoverable paths instead of walls of text.
  • Approvers decide faster when the UI answers “blast radius, peers, and deltas” before they click—context is the real throughput lever.
  • Role-based templates reduce cognitive load for requesters and shrink the ambiguity that creates over-provisioning.
  • Security and usability compound when audit evidence is designed—not exported as an afterthought.
