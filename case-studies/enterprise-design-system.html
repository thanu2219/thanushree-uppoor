Enterprise design system

Scalable design system for Nike’s internal platform ecosystem—tokens, components, documentation, and governance so 20+ applications could ship consistent, accessible product faster.

Role Design system lead
Scope 20+ applications
Users 10,000+ employees
My role

I led UX and UI on this work from discovery through delivery and design QA, alongside product, engineering, and stakeholders as described later in this case study.

TL;DR

Lead UX/UI designer for Nike’s internal design system across 20+ applications and 10,000+ employees. I shipped tokens, 60+ components, paired Figma + React libraries, documentation, and governance—then scaled adoption through pilots and contribution rules. Headline results: ~50% faster design–dev handoff, ~85% adoption among platform teams within six months, ~60% → ~95% accessibility compliance, and a large estimated savings in duplicated engineering effort.

Problem & context

Dozens of internal applications had diverged into local pattern libraries and one-off components. Employees felt the seams when switching tools; designers sank an outsized share of time into rebuilding the same tables, filters, and empty states; engineers shipped duplicate React and non-React implementations; onboarding was slow because “how we build UI here” lived in tribal knowledge. Accessibility debt compounded because there was no shared baseline—teams re-solved the same WCAG gaps in isolation.

The product goal was not a bigger sticker sheet—it was a single, enforceable source of truth that made reuse faster than improvisation for both design and engineering.

Role & responsibilities

  • I owned strategy, roadmap, and the business case—framing the system as throughput for product teams, not decoration.
  • I led the cross-program effort across 20+ apps, 15+ product teams, and React, Angular, and legacy surfaces—so the system had to be honest about incremental adoption.
  • I defined architecture and governance: how tokens, primitives, and contributions moved from proposal → review → release without bottling innovation.
  • With engineering leadership, I shaped a React reference library with API parity to design specs—the contract other stacks could align to over time.
  • I ran adoption work end to end: office hours, critiques, training tracks, and lightweight scorecards so we measured time saved and defect reduction, not component counts alone.
  • I drove accessibility as a default: WCAG 2.1 AA expectations embedded in component acceptance criteria and review checklists—supporting the compliance lift reported in outcomes.

Constraints

  • Support legacy applications while enabling new development.
  • Multiple stacks: React, Angular, and legacy systems.
  • Diverse team skill levels and design maturity.
  • Limited resources for long-term maintenance and updates.
  • Backward compatibility during migration.
  • Balancing flexibility vs. consistency across use cases.

Research & insights

  • I audited live UI across internal platforms and catalogued duplicate patterns and variant sprawl.
  • I interviewed 40+ designers and engineers and synthesized recurring pain into three themes: handoff ambiguity (states, spacing, responsive rules), reuse without guardrails (every team’s “special” table), and review fatigue (Figma and production drifting apart).
  • I benchmarked public systems (e.g. Material, Carbon, Polaris) and studied adoption models at companies like Airbnb, IBM, and Shopify for governance patterns we could adapt—not copy wholesale.
  • I facilitated workshops with PMs and tech leads to stack-rank primitives vs. “nice-to-have” patterns against roadmap pressure.
  • I aligned accessibility work to WCAG 2.1 expectations and internal enterprise requirements before scaling components.

Design process

  1. Cross-platform audit of patterns, tokens-in-name-only debt, and hotspots.
  2. Published design principles and a pragmatic system architecture.
  3. Landed a token system for color, typography, spacing, and elevation—the spine every later component hung from.
  4. Designed foundational components (buttons, inputs, navigation, tables).
  5. Extended into complex patterns (forms, data visualization, modals).
  6. Shipped documentation with live examples and “do / don’t” guidance—not only static specs.
  7. Co-designed React component APIs, states, and props with engineering.
  8. Ran three concurrent pilots with product teams on high-traffic internal workflows; we promoted only what survived real backlog pressure.
  9. Opened contribution guidelines with quality bars and review owners.
  10. Established semantic versioning and predictable release cadence.

Key decisions

  • Primitives and contribution gates first — We sequenced tokens + core components with enforced review before chasing parity with every bespoke legacy widget.
  • React as the reference implementation — One opinionated library with API parity to specs so Angular and legacy consumers could migrate in slices instead of waiting for a big bang.
  • Pilot-first mandate, not slide-first — Three teams shipped production flows on the system before we asked the wider org to retime roadmaps around adoption.

Each call traded short-term applause for long-term leverage: fewer one-off components, less ambiguous handoff, and proof that the system survived real release trains—not only critique rooms.

How I worked with engineering & product

  • I paired on naming, props, and state matrices so components reflected service reality—not only static frames.
  • I ran joint critiques with tech leads on accessibility and performance for dense enterprise tables.
  • I negotiated migration slices with PMs so adoption shipped as roadmap increments instead of vapor “UI refresh” promises.

What we explicitly de-prioritized

After the audit and token foundation landed, we still said no to an exhaustive illustration system and marketing-site-level polish for internal tools—real requests, but they would have starved the primitives that unlocked reuse. That sequencing matched the pilot plan in step 8: prove value on high-traffic flows before expanding surface area.

What shipped

A unified internal kit: 60+ components, token-driven theming, a Figma library, a React library with documented APIs, contribution paths, and release hygiene. Acceptance for net-new components explicitly referenced WCAG 2.1 AA expectations so accessibility advanced with the library—not as a downstream audit scramble.

Outcomes

The figures below blend program-level tracking (adoption surveys, quarterly efficiency pulses, design–dev cycle reviews) with engineering rollups where noted. They reflect directional impact across the internal platform footprint rather than a single product line.

40% improved design and engineering efficiency across teams
50% reduction in design-to-development handoff time
85% adoption across internal platform teams within six months
15+ product teams shipping features faster with shared patterns
70% reduction in duplicated components
60% → 95% accessibility compliance across products
60% faster onboarding for new designers and engineers
10,000+ engineering hours saved annually (engineering estimate) through reuse

Learnings

  • Foundations and documentation beat a maximal catalog for earning trust early.
  • Governance only works when it is faster to comply than to route around—pair process with tooling and eng hooks.
  • Adoption is a product problem: pilots, training, and visible wins beat mandate slides.
  • Measure outcomes teams feel (handoff time, defects, onboarding), not vanity component counts.
  • Co-own APIs with engineering from day one—your “system” is whatever ships in npm, not only Figma.
