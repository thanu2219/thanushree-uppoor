Store performance platform & BI dashboard

End-to-end 0→1 platform for Nike’s global retail operations: unified performance tracking, store workflows, and leadership visibility—replacing a fragmented licensed stack with one localized system for 10,000+ users across regions.

Role Lead UX designer
Scope End-to-end 0→1
Users 10,000+ globally
My role

I was the sole designer for UX and UI on this work—discovery through delivery and design QA—alongside product, engineering, and stakeholders as described later in this case study.

TL;DR

Sole UX/UI design lead on an end-to-end 0→1 platform unifying store performance, operational routines, and multi-level BI for Nike’s global retail footprint. We replaced a licensed patchwork with one localized product for 10,000+ users—delivering ~$6.5M annual operational savings, ~$5M TCO reduction, materially higher task adherence and data completeness, and faster weekly reporting for managers.

Problem & context

Global retail operations lacked a single place to run performance tracking, operational workflows, and leadership visibility. Teams stitched together disconnected tools and manual routines—so reporting was inconsistent, regional directors had weak real-time signal, and the stack could not scale or localize cleanly. Data completeness sat around 55%; task adherence lagged; managers burned 3–5 hours weekly on manual reporting. The organization was paying for a licensed tool that still did not unify how stores actually worked.

Why this mattered: unify workflows, retire redundant license cost, and give 10,000+ users real-time visibility across markets—without forcing a costly “translate the mess later” localization retrofit.

Role & responsibilities

  • I owned end-to-end product strategy and design for a business-critical platform, shaping roadmap tradeoffs with product leadership.
  • I defined the product vision and information architecture for a multi-role system: store employees, managers, regional directors, and executives—each with different cadence, literacy, and risk tolerance.
  • I drove the unification strategy—building the case to retire fragmented licensed tools in favor of one integrated platform with clearer ownership and lower TCO.
  • I partnered with Product, Engineering, Analytics, and regional operations leaders across APAC and EMEA to keep requirements grounded in how stores run, not only how dashboards look.
  • I designed a three-layer architecture: employee workflows (checklists, scoring, light gamification), manager tooling (alerts, adherence, operational tracking), and executive BI (trends, filters, comparative views across the Employee → Store → Region → Global hierarchy).
  • I championed localization-first IA and components so global rollout did not require a late-breaking redesign tax.
  • I led cross-regional alignment through reviews, workshops, and stakeholder readouts—translating field research into decisions executives could defend.
  • I instrumented design success beyond screens: task adherence, data completeness, reporting time, and satisfaction signals post-launch.

Constraints

  • Replace an existing licensed tool without breaking critical store workflows.
  • Support divergent roles—from floor routines to executive summaries.
  • Global rollout demanded localization and regional customization from day one.
  • Deep integration with legacy Nike systems and heterogeneous data sources.
  • Performance and freshness requirements on large, near-real-time datasets.
  • Minimal training time at the store edge.

Research & insights

I ran 35+ interviews with employees, managers, and regional leaders, shadowed store managers, and mapped three journeys—employee routines, manager tracking, and executive reporting. Two quantitative anchors kept us honest: 55% data completeness and 3–5 hours weekly lost to manual reporting before the redesign.

Field signal

Managers did not need more tiles—they needed confidence that the numbers matched how stores actually executed tasks that week.

Leadership signal

Directors wanted comparable views across regions without exporting five spreadsheets; latency and definition drift eroded trust faster than visual polish.

  • Benchmarked enterprise BI patterns (Tableau, Power BI, Looker) for density, drill behavior, and governance—not for copy-paste visuals.
  • Co-designed workshops with regional operations to pressure-test scoring, alerts, and escalation paths against real escalation habits.

Design process

  1. Discovery research across employees, managers, and leadership use cases.
  2. Mapped the operating hierarchy Employee → Store → Region → Global to navigation, permissions, and metric rollups.
  3. Designed employee routine flows—checklists, scoring, and lightweight gamification to improve adherence without feeling punitive.
  4. Built manager surfaces for adherence tracking, alerts, and operational follow-up.
  5. Designed multi-level BI dashboards with trends, filters, and comparative analytics tuned for regional directors vs. executives.
  6. Embedded a localization model for language and regional configuration before visual lock-in.
  7. Prototyped and usability-tested with 20+ participants across four rounds spanning key markets.
  8. Partnered with engineering on data architecture, query performance, and visualization fidelity under real data volumes.
  9. Shipped onboarding and training flows sized for limited floor time.

Key decisions

  • One integrated platform — Consolidated disconnected tools into a single product so workflows, metrics, and ownership stayed aligned.
  • Scoring that supports behavior — Used transparent checklists and gamified scoring to raise adherence without hiding accountability.
  • Multi-level BI, role-aware — Built leadership dashboards that respected depth for operators and scanability for executives—same data model, different lenses.
  • Localization as a design constraint — Treated language, formats, and regional rules as first-class inputs—not a post-launch retrofit.

Each decision traded short-term feature sprawl for a platform that could scale globally, retire license drag, and keep field trust in the numbers.

How I worked with data & engineering

  • I co-defined metric definitions, chart grammar, and filter semantics with analytics so labels matched warehouse truth—reducing “metric mismatch” churn.
  • I prototyped interaction and performance budgets for dense tables and near-real-time views with engineers before visual polish locked.
  • I facilitated working sessions with operations so alerts, escalations, and manager tooling mirrored how issues actually move in stores.

What we cut (on purpose)

A “build your own dashboard” playground for every store role. It sounded empowering, but it would have recreated fragmentation we were trying to retire. We shipped opinionated templates, saved views, and guardrails first—consistent with the incremental rollout and training plan stores could absorb.

What shipped

Three coordinated layers: employee tools (daily checklists, scoring, lightweight gamification), manager tools (team tracking, alerts, workflow management), and BI dashboards for regional and executive visibility with trends, filters, and comparisons—all following the Employee → Store → Region → Global hierarchy and a localization system built for APAC/EMEA scale.

Outcomes

Metrics blend finance and TCO modeling, operational telemetry (adherence, completeness), time-in-tool studies, and a post-launch satisfaction survey. Figures are directional across the global footprint rather than a single pilot store cohort.

$6.5M annual operational savings from unified operations (program estimate)
$5M total cost of ownership reduction replacing the licensed stack
+30% task adherence (65% → 95% on tracked routines)
40–60% faster reporting workflows (~2–3 hours saved weekly for managers)
55% → 95% data completeness as routines moved into the platform
10,000+ users across APAC and EMEA in rollout scope
92% user satisfaction in post-launch survey (program sample)
Real-time leadership visibility across global operations on shared definitions

Learnings

  • Enterprise retail products have to design for employees, managers, and leaders simultaneously—navigation and mental models must stay coherent across layers.
  • Transparent scoring beats mystery metrics for sustained adherence in the field.
  • Localization-first IA prevents expensive rework when formats, language, and regional rules diverge.
  • Executive BI still needs honest drill paths; depth for operators and clarity for leaders can coexist if the hierarchy is disciplined.
  • Incremental rollout, templates, and training matter as much as the dashboard pixels—especially when retiring a licensed incumbent.
