Organization hierarchy & navigation platform

CTO Office initiative (2025–2026): enterprise org visualization for Nike’s technology organization during a major restructuring—1000+ nodes, 10,000+ employees, and tooling that finally matched the complexity of how teams actually connect.

Role Product manager · Lead UI/UX
Scope 2025–2026 · CTO-backed · 1000+ nodes
Users 10,000+ employees
My role

I worked as product manager and lead UI/UX designer on this initiative from discovery through delivery and design QA, alongside engineering, data, and stakeholders as described later in this case study.

TL;DR

As product manager and lead UI/UX designer on a CTO Office initiative to visualize Nike’s technology org during a major restructuring—1000+ nodes and 10,000+ employees. I shipped hierarchy rules, progressive disclosure, clustering, search, and performance-minded interactions so leaders could navigate complexity instead of exporting PDFs. Headline outcomes: ~80% faster navigation, 5 minutes → under 30 seconds to find people, and ~+50% lift in onboarding clarity signals.

Problem & context

Nike’s technology organization spans 10,000+ people arranged in deep hierarchies—1000+ nodes in the structures we had to render. During a CTO-led restructuring, static PDFs and legacy org tools could not carry the story of who owned what, who reported where, or how teams connected. Leaders lost minutes on every lookup; new hires reported confusion; HR fielded the same org-structure questions on loop. The product had to balance political sensitivity, privacy, and the brute-force reality of rendering and searching at enterprise scale.

Why this mattered: give leadership trustworthy visibility and give employees fast discovery—so restructuring conversations happened on current data, not stale slides.

Role & responsibilities

  • I partnered directly with the CTO Office and engineering leadership to align UX bets with restructuring timelines and technical risk.
  • I drove end-to-end UX and information architecture—from hierarchy model to search, filters, and detail surfaces.
  • I defined hierarchy rules and scaffolding: Departments → Teams → Roles → Individuals so depth and breadth stayed legible instead of collapsing into hairballs.
  • I designed progressive disclosure for 1000+ node graphs—expand, collapse, focus, and contextual panels that preserved orientation.
  • I introduced clustering for oversized branches so leaders could scan spans without losing the ability to drill into exceptions.
  • I built interaction flows for drill-down, cross-team navigation, and comparison without implying surveillance of individuals.
  • I designed node cards, connectors, and detail panels so “who is this person in context?” was always one predictable gesture away.
  • I stayed embedded with engineering on rendering strategy, lazy loading, and performance budgets—because latency at this scale reads as broken data.

Constraints

  • Visualize 1000+ nodes without cognitive collapse.
  • Balance depth vs. breadth in hierarchy display and navigation.
  • Real-time or near-real-time rendering expectations on large graphs.
  • Privacy and permission boundaries on employee data.
  • Integration with HR systems and directory sources (e.g. Active Directory).
  • Responsive behavior across desktop and mobile leadership workflows.
  • Aggressive timeline tied to restructuring communications.

Research & insights

I ran structured discovery with the CTO Office, interviewed 40+ employees, managers, and executives, and analyzed how static PDFs and legacy tools failed during reorgs.

Leadership job

Executives needed the narrative of span and ownership—not a decorative graph that obscured accountability.

Operator job

Managers needed appliance-grade search: keyboard-first, tolerant of partial names, and resilient to imperfect HR data freshness.

  • Benchmarked enterprise org experiences (Workday, BambooHR patterns) for density, drill behavior, and permission-aware disclosure.
  • Stress-tested IA with card sorting and tree testing before visual polish locked.
  • Documented mental models for “explore structure” vs. “find this person now” so navigation modes did not fight each other.

Design process

  1. CTO Office discovery to lock business outcomes, risks, and release windows.
  2. Mapped the canonical hierarchy Departments → Teams → Roles → Individuals to navigation, permissions, and search indexing.
  3. Authored hierarchy rules that traded depth vs. breadth on purpose—not by accident.
  4. Designed progressive disclosure: expand/collapse, focus modes, and grouping for dense subgraphs.
  5. Introduced clustering for large departments and mega-teams.
  6. Designed drill-down and cross-team navigation flows with clear back paths.
  7. Built the node card system, connectors, and detail panels for person context.
  8. Shipped filters, autocomplete search, and discovery affordances tuned for partial information.
  9. Prototyped hierarchy visuals—including dark styling for long leadership sessions— with performance budgets in the loop.
  10. Tested with leadership and IC cohorts across multiple rounds before launch.

Key decisions

  • Explicit hierarchy rules — Codified when to show depth vs. breadth so the graph answered leadership questions instead of inventing new layouts per meeting.
  • Progressive disclosure as default — Treated 1000+ nodes as a navigation problem, not a zoom-level problem; reveal detail on intent, not on page load.
  • Clustering for mega-structures — Grouped oversized branches to protect scanability while preserving drill paths into exceptions.
  • Performance as a design requirement — Paired UX with incremental loading, indexing, and rendering strategies so “enterprise scale” stayed believable in live demos and live data.

Each decision favored trustworthy comprehension over spectacle—leaders had to trust the map during sensitive org conversations.

How I worked with leadership, HR & engineering

  • I facilitated scenario walkthroughs with CTO stakeholders and HR partners so the UI always reflected what we were allowed to show—and when.
  • I co-owned technical spikes on graph rendering, clustering, and search indexing with engineering to keep interactions within agreed latency budgets.
  • I sequenced releases with PMs: credible search and detail panels before expanding exploratory map modes that could spike support questions.

What we cut (on purpose)

Cinematic graph effects and “demo-first” interactions that looked impressive but taxed rendering and distracted from accountability. We kept the experience sober, fast, and explainable—especially while restructuring communications were still landing.

What shipped

An enterprise org platform with an interactive hierarchy graph (expand/collapse, progressive disclosure), cluster views for oversized branches, a node + connector system with rich person cards, a detail panel for role and team context, and search with autocomplete and filters. Cross-team navigation stayed anchored to the Departments → Teams → Roles → Individuals model, with dark-mode-ready visuals and optimizations for large, frequently updated trees.

Outcomes

Metrics combine instrumented task studies (navigation and search), HR inquiry volume, onboarding surveys, and post-launch satisfaction samples across leadership and IC cohorts—reported at the program level for the restructuring window.

80% faster navigation across complex structures (program measurement)
5 min → <30 sec median person search time after launch
+50% onboarding clarity lift from new hire feedback studies
1000+ node structures rendered with interactive performance targets met
88% satisfaction among leadership and employee survey respondents
Real-time org updates tied to trusted HR/AD sources during the restructuring window
9,500+ active users within the first three months post-launch
65% reduction in HR inquiries about org structure (ticket trend)

Learnings

  • Progressive disclosure is non-optional at 1000+ nodes—without it, users blame the data when the UI is what’s unreadable.
  • Depth vs. breadth is a design rule set, not a one-time layout tweak—document it and socialize it with leadership.
  • Clustering buys comprehension; pair it with crisp drill paths so detail is never lost.
  • Performance and trust move together—co-design rendering plans with engineering from day zero.
  • Real-time fidelity during reorgs matters as much as pixels; stale org charts erode confidence faster than muted colors ever could.
